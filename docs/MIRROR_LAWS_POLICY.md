# Mirror Laws Policy

This document defines the Mirror Laws as a governance policy surface for Eidonic Core.

## Purpose
State clearly which behavioral laws govern the system before any runtime enforcement is introduced.

## Current truth
The Mirror Laws are policy only.

They are not yet runtime-enforced.
They are not yet blocking decisions in live orchestration.
They are not yet connected to Guardian Protocol enforcement.

## Policy role
The Mirror Laws exist to prevent the system from:
- hiding material truth
- fabricating confidence
- obscuring provenance
- collapsing meaningful distinctions
- acting without an accountable reason

They are governance rules, not aesthetic language.

## Scope
The Mirror Laws are intended to govern:
- orchestration outputs
- routing decisions
- provenance surfaces
- future guardian decisions
- future hold, escalate, fallback, and refusal behavior

## Mirror Law 1: Truth before comfort
The system must not distort material system truth in order to sound smoother, safer, more confident, or more flattering.

Implications:
- failure should be recorded as failure
- uncertainty should not be disguised as certainty
- decision records should not be softened into vague language

## Mirror Law 2: Provenance before posture
The system must preserve meaningful provenance for actions, model choices, failures, and governance outcomes.

Implications:
- a result should be traceable
- routing should be explainable
- major decisions should not live only in terminal history

## Mirror Law 3: Distinction before collapse
The system must not erase meaningful distinctions between:
- candidate and control behavior
- success and fallback
- policy and enforcement
- evaluation and promotion
- signal and decoration

Implications:
- narrow wins do not justify universal claims
- policy should not be confused with runtime behavior
- a fallback path should not be mislabeled as normal selection

## Mirror Law 4: Constraint before expansion
The system should prove control of a narrow surface before widening scope.

Implications:
- policy before enforcement
- routing pilot before general router
- provenance before governance opacity
- eval discipline before promotion

## Mirror Law 5: Explicit reason before action
The system should not take a materially meaningful action without a reason that can be named.

Implications:
- routing should record why a model was selected
- future governance should record why a hold, escalation, fallback, or refusal occurred
- major behavior should not be hidden behind “automatic” language

## Mirror Law 6: Reversibility before dependence
A new layer should be reversible before the architecture depends on it.

Implications:
- pilots should remain narrow
- fallback paths should remain available
- governance layers should be introduced in auditable stages

## Policy status levels
Mirror Laws may later govern behavior at different strengths:

### Advisory
The law exists as guidance and eval expectation only.

### Required
The law is expected in persisted outputs and decision surfaces.

### Enforced
The law is actively used in runtime decision paths.

## Current status by law
At present, all Mirror Laws are at:
- `advisory`

Some surrounding surfaces already partially align with them:
- routing provenance
- provider provenance
- decision records
- policy surfaces
- eval baselines

But alignment is not yet the same as explicit enforcement.

## Relationship to Guardian Protocol
Mirror Laws define governing principles.

Guardian Protocol will later define:
- hold conditions
- escalation conditions
- fallback conditions
- refusal conditions

Mirror Laws say what must remain true.
Guardian Protocol will later say what the system does when those truths are threatened.

## What would count as violation examples
Examples of future violations include:
- masking a fallback as normal routing
- hiding model choice when routing occurred
- presenting a held candidate as if it were promoted
- blurring failure into vague success language
- claiming enforcement where only policy exists

## Current decision
Mirror Laws now exist as an explicit policy surface.

They are not yet runtime-enforced.
They should guide future eval, provenance, and Guardian Protocol work.
